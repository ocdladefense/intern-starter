You and Your Client [edit]⇲

By Anna Sammons and Max Mizejewski

This chapter is dedicated to the late John “JP” Powers, who passed away from Acute Myeloid Leukemia in early 2017. He joined the Oregon State Bar in September of 1974 as one of the youngest individuals ever admitted to the practice of law in Oregon, and owned his own law practice in Portland specializing in DUII defense. According to his obituary, JP looked for ways to help his clients “find serenity in their lives in a much broader sense, including through introduction to AA. He often quietly extended his generosity without any expectation of recognition, fanfare, or payment. JP’s kindness extended in every direction to those fortunate enough to meet him.”[1]

“The good physician treats the disease; the great physician
 treats the patient who has the disease.”

—William Osler

I. Introduction [edit]⇲

John Powers, who authored the original version of this chapter some years ago, noted that more people are arrested for DUII than any other crime. He speculated that everyone who practices criminal law eventually represents someone accused of it. A number of remarkable statistics regarding the prevalence of drunk driving support his conjecture.[2]

  • Approximately 1.5 million people are arrested every year for driving under the influence of alcohol and/or drugs. That means every year one out of every 121 licensed drivers is arrested for DUII.

  • On average, a person convicted of DUII has driven over the legal limit 80 times before his or her first arrest. The obvious implication of this statistic is that huge numbers of people have driven under the influence of alcohol and/or drugs at some point in their lives and made it home safely and without being arrested.

  • About one in three Americans will be involved in a DUII-related crash during their lifetimes.

DUII cases cut across all social classes and, as anyone who has conducted a voir dire in a DUII trial can attest, drunk or otherwise impaired driving touches virtually everyone’s life. The optimist will interpret these statistics as a reason to expand their DUII practice—look at all the potential clients! But what is the best way to approach this population?

The Powers Principles go a long way toward answering this question and reflect our own aspirations of how we want to be treating our clients—like fellow human beings worthy of compassion and kindness. In section II, we review the 10 Powers Principles. In section III, we share some additional practical tips for developing a more person-centered practice. In section IV, we reprint Jennifer Gerstenzang’s useful article, recently published in The Champion, about counseling addicted clients. A person’s arrest on DUII charges could very well be their “rock bottom” in the journey to overcoming addiction, and our interaction with him or her a rare opportunity to guide them toward sobriety. As attorneys, we have an ethical obligation to learn the relevant skills in this regard.

Section V is about concentration, presence and awareness. Whether we realize it or not, exposure to the constant barrage of people’s problems and the highly adversarial nature of litigation can really wear us down. It’s no secret that criminal defense attorneys suffer disproportionately from anxiety, depression and the same alcohol and substance abuse issues that plague our DUII clients. Mindfulness and meditation may not be the whole answer, but they have been shown to be helpful to many attorneys and clients. As an added bonus, increasing presence through mind-training practices can help improve our performance in court, and can help keep our clients calm during stressful proceedings.

II. The 10 (Gently Updated) Powers Principles [edit]⇲

1. Be Prepared [edit]⇲

You know what to do, but does your staff? Since you probably will not be involved in the initial call, your staff must know what information is essential to gather. Have a sample “cheat sheet” (see Chapter 3 for helpful checklists) that will suffice until your staff is familiar with the various issues and deadlines. However, the mere gathering of information will be of no use unless your staff knows how to apply the rules.

The most important area of concern is the correct arrest date so that an In-Person DMV Hearing can be requested by 5:00 p.m. on the tenth day following the arrest. For example, it’s 4:00 p.m. on Tuesday, February 18, the day after President’s Day. A prospective client calls and informs your receptionist that they were arrested on Wednesday, February 5. “Sorry, you’re too late to request a DMV hearing.” Wrong! Since the tenth day fell on a weekend and Monday was a holiday, the tenth day would be the 18th. This means your staff needs to get a request in by 5:00 p.m.

In many cases, you will be retained before your client’s arraignment. If the case is a misdemeanor, a waiver of appearance is allowed by ORS 135.030. If the case is a felony then your client must appear in person. In many cases, clients will be arrested but not booked at the jail. ORS 813.017 requires the court to ensure the person is booked after being charged with DUII—“When a person is arraigned on a charge of driving while under the influence of intoxicants in violation of ORS 813.010, a court shall ensure that the defendant submits to booking, if the person has not already been booked on that charge. [2015 c.145 §2].” If your client was not booked when they were arrested, make sure they know they will have to have their fingerprints taken in the jurisdiction of their arrest. Depending on the jurisdiction a photograph (mugshot) may be taken as well, which will likely be posted online. This is one of the greatest collateral consequences of DUII for many people. See Chapter 3 for more information on arraignments and mugshot removal.

Likewise, you should prepare your clients for the possibility that they will not be arraigned on the originally scheduled date, and that this does not necessarily mean their case is getting dismissed.
This often happens on a DRE DUII if the prosecutor wants to wait for toxicology before making a filing decision. If this happens, remember to get their citation stamped to prove they appeared in case the prosecutor attempts to file charges past the statute of limitations date. We also suggest that you send a letter to the prosecutor so that your client can arrange a voluntary appearance if and when charges are filed.

2. Quoting Prices Over the Phone [edit]⇲

You are selling your services and your expertise and may not want to quote a price over the phone. It takes time to explain your fee. Without the proper background, it could seem too expensive. Prospective clients may feel like you are trying to take advantage of them.

On the other hand, it sometimes makes sense to provide at least some price information. The prospective client may well be going down a list of search results trying to determine who to meet with, and failure to provide information may discourage them from meeting with you.

3. Schedule the Initial Meeting as Soon as Possible [edit]⇲

When people want an attorney they usually want to meet with someone immediately to reduce their anxiety and get some answers. Wherever you meet, avoid interruptions. If you’re busy with other work and believe your client will be impressed with your diligence—guess again. They made a sacrifice to meet with you and you owe them your undivided attention. The importance of presence and undivided attention will be discussed further in secton V of this chapter.

If a timely in-person meeting is not in the cards (for example, because a client is calling from out of state) make time for an uninterrupted, detailed phone consultation.

4. Try to Ascertain the Client’s Emotional Priorities During your Initial Meeting [edit]⇲

What is most important to this person? Your concept of the best method of handling the case might be totally different than the client’s. Instead of talking about the law (which the client trusts you know), ask about their expectations. What would ultimately make them most happy?

5. Establish your Client’s Expectations — and Exceed Them [edit]⇲

Thoroughly discuss how you propose to handle the matter and what your client can expect in terms of results and costs. If you discover that you are unable to meet your commitments or that the results are not as anticipated, discuss the details with the client as soon as possible. Under-promise and over-deliver is a good mantra in the legal profession.

6. Memorialize Your Agreement in Writing and Send a Letter of Understanding and Thanks [edit]⇲

At the conclusion of the initial interview, Mr. Powers provided his prospective clients with a packet of information and always followed up with a letter expressing his commitment, a copy of the fee agreement, etc. Politely worded engagement letters immediately set a courteous, professional tone.

7. Promptly Return Phone Calls, Emails and Other Communications [edit]⇲

The one guarantee we should all make to every client: “I will return your call within 24 hours. Myself.”

8. Communicate with Your Client in the Manner They Prefer [edit]⇲

Some clients prefer written letters and phone calls, many prefer text and/or email. Find out what they prefer up front. Be flexible if you can, but set some boundaries.

9. Treat your Clients Like You Would Want to Be Treated Yourself [edit]⇲

DUII clients can be very challenging. The good old Golden Rule goes a long way in establishing a good rapport. And, if you had a “bad” day and were short with your client, don’t be afraid to promptly apologize.

10. Be Objective with Regard to your Client’s Needs [edit]⇲

A great number of DUII clients are alcoholics. Alcoholism is a chronic, progressive disease with three stages. Fortunately, we seldom see anyone in the third stage. If you have seen someone in this last condition, you’ll never forget it.

Unfortunately, most clients are in the first stage which is not easy to identify.

We’re lawyers, not doctors, and we do not possess the expertise to diagnose anyone. However, if you familiarize yourself with some aspects of the disease and relate those to your client in a caring, compassionate manner, you might be instrumental in cutting through their “denial” and helping them get treatment. For more on speaking with clients about alcoholism and addiction, see
section IV.

III. The Importance of Unconditional Positive Regard [edit]⇲

Powers is of course correct in noting that we are lawyers and not doctors. But judges do call us counselors.

“Thank you, counselor, I have your argument.”

Because of the casual way this word is used in our profession, the deeper meaning can sometimes fail to register. But according to our licenses, and consistent with the reality of our collective experience, we are all counselors at law—“counselors” first and “at law” second.

What does it mean to be a counselor? It means our clients hire us to reduce their suffering. They want us to be “real” with them, to inject humanity into an inhuman process, to put ourselves in their shoes, view the issues through their eyes, and reduce their pain by focusing on their needs, desires, and values. Even the most cursory look at online client reviews teaches us that clients judge us based on how we make them feel, not necessarily on the quality of our legal representation. Relating to our clients in a way that reduces their suffering is a prerequisite for doing a truly excellent job.

The Powers Principles immediately ring true because each one is aimed at reducing client suffering—by explaining what is going on and what clients can expect in the future, by making sure they do not feel taken advantage of financially, and by showing interest in their concerns and perspectives. In legal theory, this type of counseling-inspired, client-centered approach was first advocated by Binder and Price in the late 1970s and has become standard among frontline legal defenders.[3]

Client-centered advocacy traces back to Carl Rogers (1902–1987), the most influential proponent of person-centered psychological counseling. He explained the “central hypothesis” of his approach as follows: “Individuals have within themselves vast resources for self-understanding and for altering their self-concept, attitudes, and self-directed behavior – [but] these resources can be tapped only if a definable climate of facilitative psychological attitudes can be provided.”[4]

Rogers identified three conditions for creating that climate in therapy, and they apply with equal force to person-centered legal representation.

First, Rogers extolled a counselor’s Realness or “genuineness,” explaining that the more a counselor is him or herself in the relationship, putting up no professional front or façade, the better the working relationship is likely to be.

Second, he encouraged Empathy—which he defined as actively listening with true interest and, thereby, accurately understanding a client’s feelings and personal meaning. In the world of criminal defense, clients’ feelings often revolve around the pain of lost control. Clients frequently hire us to restore a sense of perceived control, which has been shown to correlate with physical health and subjective feelings of well-being. This is why things like letters of engagement and other signs of professionalism and respect, careful explanations of criminal and administrative procedures, detailed discussions of the evidence and the like can be so important and valuable to clients, even though it may feel (to us) like these activities do not contribute directly toward a favorable resolution or victory at trial.

Perhaps most relevant to attorneys is Rogers’ third condition for creating the right climate—an attitude of Unconditional Positive Regard. He described it as follows:

To the extent that the counselor finds himself or herself experiencing warm acceptance of each aspect of the clients experience as being part of that client, he or she is experiencing unconditional positive regard.… This means there are no conditions of acceptance, no feeling of “I only like you if you are thus and so.” It means “prizing” of the person.… It is at the opposite pole from a selective evaluating attitude – “You are bad in these ways, good in those.” It involves as much feeling of acceptance for the client’s expression of negative, “bad”…defensive, abnormal feelings as for his expression of “good” [ones]…

Rogers gave some examples of thoughts that are characteristic of unconditional positive regard; his list serves as a useful tool for assessing one’s own attitude:

  • I feel no revulsion at anything the client says.

  • I feel neither approval nor disapproval of the client and his statements – simply acceptance.

  • I feel warmly toward the client, toward his weaknesses and problems as well as his potentialities.

  • I am not inclined to pass judgment on what the client tells me.

  • I like my client.

Developing unconditional positive regard even toward difficult clients is a matter of intentionality. Some practitioners choose to reframe difficult situations, such as that of somebody facing a fifth or sixth DUII, viewing the broader context of where a person is coming from. Others approach the issue from philosophical or religious perspectives. There is no one right approach.

In our desire to genuinely connect with clients, some of us fail to develop appropriate professional boundaries. These signs of unhealthy boundaries may be helpful for purposes of a self-check:

  • Excessive self-disclosure to client.

  • Feeling “overwhelmed” by client.

  • Allowing a client to dictate the direction of the case.

  • Going against personal values to “help” client.

Practice Tip

Need to create better boundaries? Here are some tips:

  • Proper boundaries begin at home.

Consider physically putting away your devices and going completely offline during regularly scheduled hours, such as evenings, early mornings, or one weekend day a week.

  • Create structure.

For instance, let’s say your client has a habit of calling at all hours to discuss their feelings about the case. Clearly scheduled weekly check-ins can eliminate this problem.

  • Lay out your limits very clearly.

For instance, tell your clients verbally (or, better yet, in writing) the hours you will be available for work-related conversations, what constitutes an emergency, and what they should do in an emergency. Explain your boundaries in concrete terms and relate them to your clients’ interests. For example, you can say: “I may not be able to get back to you this week because I am working on another case. But please rest assured that when the time comes for me to focus on your case, it, too, will have my undivided attention.”

  • Plan in advance for things to go wrong.

It’s helpful to visualize your boundaries getting crossed, and how you’re going to handle those situations beforehand so you have a plan of action. This way, when a difficult situation comes up, you won’t be hijacked by your emotions.

  • Bring up problems right away.

When boundaries are violated, it’s not uncommon for people to get upset and ruminate endlessly. Instead, plan to speak up immediately.

To sum up, there is no magic formula for striking the right balance of realness, empathy, unconditional positive regard and appropriate professional boundaries. But having an awareness of what an excellent attorney-client relationship ought to look like and what aspects of the relationship we ought to be paying attention to can help us all advance toward that goal.

IV. Counseling the Addicted Client: A Responsible and Restorative Practice [edit]⇲

Jennifer Gerstenzang, The Champion, August 2018. © 2018, Natonal Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers. Reprinted with permission.

Defense Attorneys Have The Authority And Ability To Initiate Change [edit]⇲

Defense attorneys hold the knowledge and skill to help clients navigate the criminal justice system. For many clients, this is the first time they have ever been in trouble with the law, and it is a haunting and traumatic experience. They cling to counsel’s words and advice in the belief that the lawyer holds the secret to their fate. Because their freedom is at stake, they are highly motivated to achieve sobriety with a commitment and focus not previously possessed.

For this reason, a client is willing to heed counsel’s advice and direction, even if the client has faithfully ignored this same advice for years. As such, attorneys have a duty to make sure the advice they offer is sound and is tailored to the client’s specific needs. In order to do that, attorneys must stay informed and up to date on the new and changing research and treatments for addiction. In this way, they can best serve their clients as they embark on their journey toward sobriety.

Attorneys who limit their practice solely to representing the client on the criminal charges are missing an opportunity to develop a deeper lawyer-client relationship and to help their client avoid future issues with the law and in the client’s personal life. The ABA Model Rules are not explicit when it comes to handling a client with an addiction, but they provide guidance. Notable model rules include Rule 1.1, discussing attorney competence[5] when representing a client; Rule 1.14, discussing representing a client with diminished capacity;[6] and Rule 1.6, discussing duties of confidentiality to the client.[7]

Diminished capacity is defined as “an unbalanced mental state that is considered to make a person less answerable for a crime and is recognized as grounds to reduce the charge.”[8] In cases with a client who is struggling with an addiction, quite often this information can be a source of mitigation when negotiating the case and humanizing the client to the court and opposing counsel. Rule 1.14 allows lawyers to consult with individuals or entities that have the ability to take action to protect clients when clients are unable to adequately act in their own best interests. Understanding the different treatments that are available and knowing who to consult is an important aspect of representing the client to the best of the lawyer’s ability.

Determining the Right Treatment for the Client [edit]⇲

Take the time to really speak to the client (and perhaps family members as well). Find out more about his[9] relationship with drugs or alcohol. In many DUII cases, especially involving first-time offenders, the client’s initial night in jail may serve as treatment enough. Many clients, however, require a more sophisticated approach. The clients with greater needs are usually easy to identify. For example, their blood alcohol concentrations may be quite high, they may have prior arrests, or the specific facts surrounding their arrest may alert counsel to a substance abuse disorder.

As mentioned above, it is important to talk to the client’s family members in assessing the client’s needs. Clients are not always the best historians and may be unwilling or unable to share the truth with the attorney. Family members can provide a wealth of important information, including treatments that the client has tried in the past — both successfully and unsuccessfully — and which approaches helped the client find the most relief. Additionally, they may provide the lawyer with a more complete background about when and why the client began using drugs or alcohol in the first place. Sometimes clients are too close to their addiction to see things as clearly as their loved ones. All this information can be used to develop a promising strategy to address the client’s needs while the attorney is fighting the client’s criminal case. Of course, the lawyer must make sure she speaks with the client before talking to the family to avoid breaching confidentiality.

Creating a More Intimate Attorney-Client Relationship [edit]⇲

An attorney’s genius in the courtroom will likely be appreciated much more by opposing counsel and the bench rather than the attorney’s client. Clients will not remember the attorney’s strategy, cross-examination, or impassioned argument nearly as much as they will remember the way she made them feel. The conversations the lawyer has with the client about the client’s struggle with alcohol or drugs, what this struggle has cost him, and how he can try to find relief will show the client that counsel cares about who he is and the quality of his life after the case resolves. Colleagues will refer clients to an attorney based on counsel’s legal prowess. Clients will refer their friends based on the excellence of counsel’s emotional first aid.

These conversations should start as soon as possible in the attorney-client relationship. They are powerful trust-builders and will set the tone for the rest of the relationship. The information gleaned from these conversations will help the lawyer understand the client better and make the lawyer a far better advocate on behalf of the client during negotiations or, if necessary, at any sentencing hearing. Finally, having this conversation early can help counsel get her client into treatment at the earliest possible date. Clients can be saved from custody time or a bail increase based upon their placement in a treatment facility. Early identification of a problem allows attorney and client time to find the right treatment option and gives the client the ability to make any lifestyle adjustments needed to start treatment.

Available Treatment Options [edit]⇲

New and exciting treatments exist that go beyond the familiar AA/NA 12-step approach. Luckily, in recent years there has been a general shift in the way people think and talk about addiction. “The surgeon general’s report reaffirms what the scientific establishment has been saying for years: addiction is a disease, not a moral failing. It is characterized not necessarily by physical dependence or withdrawal but by compulsive repetition of an activity despite life- damaging consequences.”[10] The same report found that 21 million Americans have a drug or alcohol addiction, which makes the disorder more common than cancer.[11] With this information, motivated scientists and researchers are examining what is actually happening in the addicted brain.

At this point in time, two schools of thought predominate regarding the causes of and best treatment practices for addiction:

1. Addiction is caused by faulty chemistry or bad wiring of the brain. Treatment should include certain medications or brain stimulation while simultaneously providing psycho-social support; or

2. Addiction treatment requires intensive psychological work that can be assisted through the use of medication to manage cravings and withdrawal symptoms.

As with every worthy goal in life, there is no silver bullet or quick solution when it comes to recovery. The options listed below can seldom be used on their own. More likely the client will require a combination of treatments or therapies. Some of these treatment options are listed below. Keep in mind that these treatments must be matched to the client based on his unique circumstances, and often they must be used in combination with other treatments.

Naltrexone/Vivitrol® [edit]⇲

Opioid receptors in the brain’s reward center are responsible for the euphoric effects individuals feel when they use opioids such as heroin and oxycodone and when they drink alcohol. “A receptor is like a button or a lock on the surface of a cell — when ‘pushed’ or ‘unlocked’ by another chemical, a reaction occurs, and a cascade of effects is set into motion.”[12] When these opioid receptors are stimulated, the user feels a rush of pleasurable and rewarding feelings.

Naltrexone is a mu-opioid receptor blocker, also known as an opioid antagonist. Thus, an individual who takes naltrexone will not get the pleasurable effects of opioids or alcohol. Imagine placing a small yet strong cap on the opioid receptors in the brain, thereby locking the door to the receptor closed. Because the receptor is unable to be stimulated, no chemical reaction can occur, and thus there is no release of the euphoric effects of the drug or alcohol.

This effectively eliminates the temptation to use. This is because if an individual does take opioids or drink alcohol while taking naltrexone, they will be unable to feel the “high” from the drugs. People struggling with alcohol addiction may not stop drinking entirely while on naltrexone, but binge-drinking is dramatically reduced. When they do not find the relief they are accustomed to in their first or second drink, they stop drinking.

Individuals can be so afraid of the pain of withdrawal that just one relapse can terminate their recovery efforts. If the individual is taking naltrexone and relapses, not only will he not get the pleasure from taking the drug, but he also will avoid the pain of withdrawal. Since the client’s brain has not processed the drug or alcohol and experienced the euphoric effect, the brain is not missing the drug or alcohol when it is once again absent from the body.

“Of great importance, persons treated with naltrexone are less likely to report alcohol and opioid cravings and the repeated thoughts and obsessions with drug use when they are not using.”[13] Anecdotally, there have also been reports that naltrexone has helped individuals struggling with other disorders, such as gambling, smoking, and obsessive-compulsive disorders. Additionally, “persons maintained on naltrexone have a very low risk of continued illegal opioid use and are less likely to drink alcohol heavily compared to placebo.”[14]

Naltrexone takes the edge off the addiction cravings while individuals are trying to pick up the other pieces of their lives. It can be very helpful for individuals who are being released from jail or prison to assist them in maintaining their sobriety while they adjust to freedom. In many cases, it is an extraordinary and effective tool, and is being adopted by many drug courts throughout the country, with promising results. In drug courts, it is used in conjunction with the other treatments and programs to help the participants succeed in reaching their goals.

Naltrexone is an alternative to other medications, like Suboxone and methadone, that are commonly used to treat opioid addiction. Recall that naltrexone is an opioid antagonist, while Suboxone and methadone are opioid agonists, which means they are substances that initiate a physiological response when combined with a receptor. Methadone is a full opioid agonist while Suboxone is a partial opioid agonist. This means that Suboxone and methadone attach themselves to opioid receptors but, instead of blocking the receptors, they stimulate the receptors. This helps individuals working on their sobriety to manage or avoid the pain from withdrawal by essentially weaning off drugs or alcohol. It can also be helpful because these drugs are supposed to be less addictive than the drug they are trying to kick.

However, methadone and Suboxone are still opioids, and thus can be addictive in themselves. For an individual who has gone through withdrawal and is clean, naltrexone is a beneficial option because it is not addictive.

Naltrexone, which must be prescribed by a doctor, can be administered in two ways. It can either be taken in pill form orally on a once-daily basis or it can be injected into the buttocks once every four weeks. The first option is economical and is more likely to be covered by insurance. The latter is ideal because the client only has to choose his sobriety once a month rather than make that choice every day. The injection, however, can be very expensive and may not be covered by insurance.

It is important that clients be prepared to receive the treatment before having either the pill or injection administered. Being prepared is important because the medication will block the opioid receptors in the brain, which can be a very good thing if they are trying to maintain their sobriety, and can be a very painful decision in some cases. Patients must stop taking any opioids for at least 7 to 14 days before starting naltrexone. If they use within that time frame, taking naltrexone can trigger sudden opioid withdrawal, which is not only painful but also can be dangerous. If the person who is on naltrexone gets injured, they will not be able to manage their pain with typical prescription pain relievers. As the receptors for these drugs are blocked, they will not receive the intended relief. In emergencies, doctors still have some options available to treat pain that will not be blocked by the receptors, but these should be reserved for serious situations.

Additionally, when using naltrexone clients should be cautioned about the dangers of overdose if and when they end their treatment. When individuals who have a history of long-term opioid use relapse, they may return to their typical dosage without accounting for the impact their sobriety had on their tolerance. This can lead to devastating, and sometimes fatal, overdoses.

A final caution is to make sure the client is simultaneously being treated for any emotional or psychological concerns when taking this medication. If the client is using substances as a form of self-medication, taking away the relief without responsibly managing the underlying pain or trauma may lead to the client simply replacing one substance with another. Naltrexone does not block the receptors for amphetamines, only opioids, and unfortunately it does not block the chemical reaction in the brain if the client uses fentanyl. This additional support may help the client avoid a panicked and desperate need for an escape, and the client will be building the tools to maintain long-term sobriety.

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) [edit]⇲

As understanding of the addicted brain expands, so do the creativity of the treatments offered. Psychiatrists and neuroscientists have observed that addiction weakens the brain’s executive control center, the prefrontal cortex. For example, in a study done by Ritz Z. Goldstein, highlighted in National Geographic,[15] she revealed that cocaine addicts have reduced gray matter volume in their prefrontal cortex. This means that addicted individuals have poorer executive function and generally perform worse in psychological tests of memory, attention, and decision- making. Unfortunately, her study does not determine whether addiction causes these impairments in the brain or if the impairments made the brain more vulnerable to addiction.

Dr. Luigi Gallimberti, a psychiatrist and toxicologist who has treated addiction for 30 years, was inspired by studies conducted by neurologist Antonello Bonci and the National Institute on Drug Abuse. Bonci found that the region in the brain responsible for inhibiting behavior in cocaine- addicted rats was abnormally quiet. When he activated these cells in the rats, their “interest in cocaine basically vanished.”[16] Gallimberti used this research to try Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) on people struggling with cocaine addiction.

TMS involves the application of electromagnetic pulses to the prefrontal cortex to activate those neurons. This form of brain stimulation has been used to treat depression and migraines. Scientists run electrical current through a coiled wire inside a wand, and the wand creates a magnetic pulse that alters electrical activity in the brain. Gallimberti hypothesized that repeated pulses may activate drug-damaged neural pathways much like a reboot on a frozen computer.

Working together alongside Gallimberti’s partner, neurocognitive psychologist Alberto Terraneo, the researchers tested their theory on 29 people addicted to cocaine. Sixteen people received TMS, while 13 received standard care, but not TMS. By the end of the trial, 11 people were drug-free in the TMS group, while only three were drug-free in the standard care group.

The research continues in this area but the initial results are promising. There are other ways that individuals can strengthen their prefrontal cortex without sending electrical currents into the brain, including mindfulness training and meditation, but these practices require discipline and dedication, which may be difficult during the time someone is struggling with addiction. Other research, listed below, looks at restoring the damaged or weakened brain more generally.

Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD) Therapy [edit]⇲

Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD) therapy is another addiction treatment that focuses on strengthening the brain. NAD is a coenzyme of niacin and is a building block in the body that the brain, internal organs, and neurological systems rely on to function well. As people age, their NAD levels decline. Addiction, depression, stress, and illness accelerate the depletion of NAD levels as well.

NAD therapy works to restore these depleted enzymes and thus boost the function of the brain. When used for addiction therapy, though it is often also used to treat depression, anxiety and other ailments as well, the treatment lasts about 10 days. Each day the patient receives an intravenous infusion of the enzyme for about 6 to 8 hours.

NAD therapy is often combined with other tools and medications mentioned in this article to increase the effectiveness of the treatment. When NAD therapy is used to help people during their initial detoxification, practitioners often use the BRIDGE to manage the patients discomfort during withdrawal.

The BRIDGE [edit]⇲

As mentioned previously, the pain of detoxification, or even merely the fear of that pain, can keep an individual from seeking treatment and sobriety. The BRIDGE is a fascinating device that helps individuals manage their pain — either pain from withdrawal or other acute pain — through neuro-stimulation. The BRIDGE sits on a patient’s ear, is minimally invasive, and is an auricular peripheral nerve field stimulator that connects topically to the patient’s ear, blocking pain signals from getting to the brain.

The benefit to this treatment, as opposed to medication assisted treatment such as Naltrexone, Methadone or Suboxone, is that the client does not need to complete his detox before using the BRIDGE. Additionally, this treatment works very quickly, with patients reporting pain reduction in as little as 20 to 30 minutes.

In practice, an individual using this device for pain reduction associated with withdrawal would wear the BRIDGE for the initial four to five days of detoxification, when the pain is the most acute and the most unbearable. Then the device would be safely removed. These types of treatments are effective at convincing individuals they will be able to handle the process of withdrawal.

Intervention [edit]⇲

Popularized by misleading and objectively bad television, interventionists are on the rise. When conducted by trained, educated and licensed professionals, interventions can be incredibly effective. A good interventionist will work with the family first, before talking to the loved one with the addiction. The interventionist can identify any dysfunction in the family dynamics and can prepare the family for what to expect and what their roles will be in their loved one’s recovery.

Often, an individual struggling with an addiction may be in a relationship with someone who, in some ways, triggers or supports the addicted behavior. In some relationships, a spouse or significant other subconsciously relies on their loved one’s addiction, and may feel as if they are losing control if their loved one seeks treatment. A great intervention program will also work closely with the enabling spouse to convince him or her to participate in the intervention and play a supportive role to get the loved one into treatment.

If the client’s family is considering an intervention, it is worth it to vet the program to see how involved people at the program are and how much time they spend with the family in advance of the actual intervention. The client will appreciate the fact that the attorney helped him and his family spend their money wisely.

Treatment Centers [edit]⇲

Remember, any recommendation an attorney makes to a client will reflect upon the attorney. Counsel would not refer a prior client to another attorney she did not know or trust, and thus she should be sure she does her research when suggesting a treatment center. She has already invested a lot of time in the client’s sobriety, and therefore she should make sure she is recommending a quality placement.

In recent years and in reaction to the explosion of the opioid epidemic, the United States has seen a huge growth in addiction treatment centers. New inpatient, outpatient, partial hospitalization, intensive outpatient, and sober living facilities are rapidly popping up throughout the country. With insurance footing the bill for many of these centers, there is a great deal of money to be made in these facilities, and regulation is surprisingly relaxed. In Malibu, California, for example, where the population is only 13,000, there are 47 licensed rehabilitation centers. In Southern California, there are 1,117 licensed rehabilitation centers, and this does not include unlicensed sober living homes.[17]

As with all professions, there may be some “bad apples.” With so much variety, and with the potential for centers to make a lot of money very quickly, some programs undoubtedly are cutting corners. Additionally, many people who start programs are in recovery themselves. This can be both a blessing and a curse. Some programs have had to shut down because of their founders relapsing in their recovery, while other programs thrive by being run by someone with an intimate understanding of recovery.

Reach out to the treatment and rehabilitation community to make a few connections. Sit down with people who are familiar with the local programs and their reputations. Counsel should find people whose opinions she trusts and learn what programs they endorse. Then contact those programs. Take a tour of their facilities. Keep a list and try to match the client’s insurance with the right program. Additionally, make sure that licensed practitioners are working with the clients on a daily basis, as some programs will hire student practitioners to do the day-to-day work.

Conclusion [edit]⇲

One person’s success in recovery does not mean that he or she has uncovered a secret formula for sobriety that can translate to another person’s success. In the same way that there are many unique paths to addiction, there are many twisting trails in the journey to sobriety. No two paths are exactly alike. Take the lessons from the success stories of clients as anecdotal evidence to pass on to future clients. These stories can be motivating and inspiring, and can give clients a realistic picture of recovery. Be careful, however, not to overly rely on one treatment model.

Finally, it is important that counsel take an active role in the client’s recovery. Check in with the client as regularly as possible to see how the treatment is going. Remember, an attorney has a unique position of authority that can motivate the client to keep pushing himself and will remind him what is at stake. If the client is not happy or successful with whatever treatment he is pursuing, it may be because it is not a good fit for him. Let the client know that he has an option of leaving or changing treatment, but that to do so, it is best, and in some cases mandatory, that he finds a new treatment option before abandoning the current treatment model.

Talk to the staff at the program the client is attending to track his progress. The client’s account of his improvement may be quite different from the program’s update — for better or for worse. Keep the client accountable and make sure he is taking an active role in his recovery.

Most DUII attorneys, and criminal defense attorneys in general, work hard because they care about their clients. Clients look to counsel to guide them through the darkest period of their lives. Ultimately, clients are responsible for their own success in rehabilitation, but their lawyers play an important role steering them in the right direction and on the right path. The coming years bring extraordinary strides in science and neurology that will only continue to help clients achieve sobriety and success. Learning about new treatments is as important as reading new case law. Doing so provides an invaluable service and assists in obtaining the best possible result for clients.

Addendum [edit]⇲

It is worth noting that some clients are going to be very resistant to formal substance abuse treatment but may be interested in and/or open to other approaches toward achieving sobriety.

Alcoholics Anonymous and related 12-step programs are informal, hugely popular higher power–based support groups that have helped countless people. In the Eugene area alone, there are multiple 12-step meetings every day—morning, noon and night—which can be found at www.eviaa.org. Clients should be able to get a home group, a sponsor, and service position without too much trouble. In terms of mitigation, this is an anonymous program so it may be difficult to get a letter of attendance; but if your client does have a sponsor, that person may be willing to write a letter or appear at a sentencing hearing. You don’t want to offend people by suggesting AA, but if they seem like they would benefit, it may be worth a mention.

Other clients may be open to private medical treatment with a psychiatrist and/or therapist. You will want to have your client sign a release of information and have a letter sent to your office. Each doctor is different and may have an approach that resonates.

Finally, there is bibliotherapy. Clients who may not be ready for more aggressive types of treatment are often willing to pick up a book. There are titles to appeal to every possible angle: from Quit Like a Woman by Holly Whitaker and The Craving Mind by Judson Brewer to books about new programs being developed that use psychedelic drugs to treat drug and alcohol addiction, for example at the Johns Hopkins Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research https://hopkinspsychedelic.org/ (see, e.g., Psychedelic Medicine: The Healing Powers of LSD, MDMA, Psilocybin and Ayahuasca by Dr. Richard Louis Miller and How to Change Your Mind by Michael Pollan).

V. Concentration and Awareness [edit]⇲

As Thich Nhat Hanh said: “The most precious gift we can offer others is our presence. When mindfulness embraces those we love, they will bloom like flowers.”[18] Henry David Thoreau also acknowledges the important of presence: “You must live in the present, launch yourself on every wave, find your eternity in each moment. Fools stand on their island of opportunities and look toward another land. There is no other land; there is no other life but this.”[19] By being more present, more open, and less reactive, we can reduce our own suffering as well as the suffering of those around us, such as our clients, coworkers and families.

Meditation and mindfulness practices aimed at achieving this sort of presence are used by many religions, but there is nothing fundamentally religious about these practices and they cross boundaries between religious and secular life. They are popular because they are simple and accessible to everyone. By paying very close attention to the present moment we can develop clarity and penetrative insight. But how do we get there?

In his useful 2018 book, Aware: The Science and Practice of Presence, Dr. Dan Siegel describes a method of cultivating presence by developing three pillars of mind training: focused attention, open awareness and kind intention.[20] Dr. Siegel has also developed the “Wheel of Awareness” meditation practice, included in Appendix A.

Focused attention is something that attorneys spend a great deal of time doing. As stated in the December 2019 issue of the Oregon State Bar Bulletin, “[Legal work] requires sustained focus, marathon-caliber mental endurance. It requires attention, the very currency of the billable hour.”[21] Focused attention is what drives energy and information into awareness. How can we improve and hone our attention?

Focused attention meditation, or concentration meditation, is a meditation practice in which we focus on something such as the breath and when our mind is inevitably distracted we bring it back again and again. This is akin to a mental workout: the loss of attention is like the relaxing of our attention, and bringing attention back to our object of meditation is like flexing the muscle of attention. Over time you may find that your ability to concentrate and have longer periods of undistracted attention will improve.

The object of focus could be internal or external, for example:

  • Mantra – repeating a word, phrase, or sound over and over.

  • Visualization – picturing a place or focusing on a goal.

  • A body part – focusing on a particular area or sensation in the body.

  • Candle – looking at a flame to focus the mind.

  • Mala beads – counting beads on a mala.

  • Sound – listening to a gong or chime.

In this type of meditation, the goal is to keep the attention focused and nothing else. So if you were to stare at a candle, or repeat a mantra, you would just engage the mind in that practice. The more you practice, the better you will become at zoning in and the fewer distractions and lapses in concentration you will experience.

Open awareness is the ability to perceive the multitude of internal and external sensory input that we experience at any given time without being distracted by any particular input and becoming lost in thought. Also called open monitoring or non-directive meditation, this style of formal meditation is the opposite of focused attention. Instead of concentrating on something, your attention is open and remains aware of everything that is happening. All experiences are perceived as they happen, with a goal of remaining non-judgmental toward oneself. Some of the things that are watched in this meditation are:

  • Thoughts

  • Feelings

  • Memories

  • Sounds

  • Smells

  • Bodily sensations

Instead of getting caught up in a thought or feeling, the practitioner is instead encouraged to simply witness everything and be present to whatever is happening.[22] This is related to the practice of Mindfulness which has gained recent popularity in the West for its ability to help people have more clarity and equanimity in day-to-day life. For example, Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) has popularized mindfulness practice and helped millions of Americans with a practical application of mindfulness.[23] Jon Kabat-Zinn succinctly defines mindfulness as paying attention on purpose, in the present moment and nonjudgmentally.[24]

Dr. Siegel’s third pillar of mind training, kind intention, dovetails with Rogers’ concept of unconditional positive regard (see section III, above). This is about our relational sense in the world and is an acknowledgment of the interconnection of all things. Knowing that you are connected to all beings allows compassion to naturally flow. Thich Nhat Hanh coined the term “interbeing” to describe this concept.[25] 

Practice Tip

The next time you know you will be dealing with a difficult client, take a few minutes to breathe and drop in to the present moment prior to the meeting. There are many great meditation apps out there that can help with this, including Insight Timer and Waking Up. I once had a client come into my office shaking uncontrollably due to her fearful internal mental simulations about going to jail. I ran a brief three-minute meditation on my phone and it brought her back into the room, into her body, and able to communicate without shaking.

Another form of meditation, which is considered the highest teaching by some ancient wisdom traditions in places such as Tibet,[26] is a direct experience of pure consciousness unencumbered by conceptual thought. In this practice there is no meditator, there is just being. Seeing without a seer, and hearing without a hearer, this is the ineffable self-transcendent experience of non-dual awareness (NDA). By turning awareness back on to itself, one can experience the mathematical space that is the formless source of all form. This may be what physicists call the “quantum vacuum.” This level of awareness has recently been introduced in the West with names such as Effortless Mindfulness[27] and Natural Awareness.[28] Non-duality is a recognition that the dualistic view of the world—inside versus outside, subject versus object, and other versus self—may not be accurate.[29] People often experience NDA when immersed in nature, listening to music or playing sports. The panoramic flow state that is experienced by an athlete when they are “in the zone” is an expression of NDA. The DUII trial lawyer who can freely access this realm has a distinct advantage in the courtroom. Not only does operating from this level of awareness dissolve the narrow concerns of the ego-identified self, it opens the door to infinite possibility.

The Wheel of Awareness, provided in Appendix A, is a simple meditation practice that incorporates all four of these concepts. Guided audio versions of the meditation are also available at drdansiegel.com. The Wheel of Awareness is a visual metaphor with a center hub that represents awareness or consciousness itself (the context of consciousness), a wheel that represents all of the things awareness can know (the contents of consciousness), and a spoke of attention that goes from the hub to the wheel.

  • Steps 3 and 4 use focused attention to methodically review the five senses and internal bodily sensations.

  • Steps 5 and 6 use open awareness to pay attention to mental life and how thoughts and emotions present themselves, stay in awareness, and disappear.

  • Step 7 provides an opportunity to experience non-dual awareness by bending the spoke of attention back into awareness itself.

  • Step 8 helps develop kind intention by sensing our relational experience with others near us and gradually moving that sense out to include all life.

VI. Conclusion [edit]⇲

Practicing DUII defense can be challenging and your relationship with your clients can be one of the main sources of both stress and joy. We hope that the information provided in this chapter will assist you both in working with your client and maintaining your own well-being during the lifecycle of your DUII case. The Powers Principles go a long way to establishing trust and rapport with your client. The practice of unconditional positive regard is a way to interact with your clients in a healthy and positive way. The article about counseling the addicted client should provide you with some tools and information to better understand the plight of a client with alcohol and addiction issues. Finally, we hope that the section on concentration and awareness will improve your ability to be flexible and open-minded when tackling a DUII case, and provide one method of self-care when things seem overwhelming. Meditation is a way to experience what your mind is like before it becomes cluttered by concepts, judgments, reactions and other contractions of consciousness. A regular meditation practice is a tool that may benefit both you and your client as you work through the triumphs and defeats of a case. Working with DUII clients can be extremely rewarding and beneficial to both the attorney and the client. This is truly a practice area where the attorney has an opportunity to change people’s lives for the better.
